Field guide: reading a page like an inspector

The address bar

  • Padlock, not just "https." Click it — the certificate should name the actual company, not a random third party.
  • Spelling that's almost right. "arnazon" or "paypa1" swap letters to fool a quick glance.
  • A pile of subdomains. secure.login.account.verify-store.com is built to look official while hiding the real host.
  • Shortened links. bit.ly and similar hide where you're actually going — expand them first, or avoid buying through one entirely.

The page itself

  • Prices far below everyone else's. A "genuine" phone at half price is the oldest trick there is.
  • No way to contact a real person. A working phone number or physical address is a good sign; a contact form only is a caution.
  • Countdown timers on everything. Manufactured urgency is designed to stop you from checking further.
  • Reviews that are all five stars, all recent. Real stores have some mixed, older reviews too.

How you're asked to pay

  • Only accepts direct EcoCash/bank transfer to a personal account. Legitimate stores use merchant accounts, not a stranger's personal wallet.
  • Asks you to pay outside the platform. If a marketplace seller pushes you off-app to "save on fees," that's a red flag.
  • No refund or return policy anywhere. Even a short one is better than none.
  • Pressures you to decide immediately. A real seller can wait five minutes while you check them out.
